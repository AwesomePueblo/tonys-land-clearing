The Process

Stump grinding is the process of using a rotating cutting wheel with carbide teeth to chip away a tree stump down to below ground level. Unlike stump removal, which pulls the entire root ball out of the ground, grinding leaves the root system in place and reduces the visible stump to a pile of wood chips and sawdust.

The result is a flat, level area where the stump used to be — filled with the ground chips — where grass or other plants can eventually grow back.

It depends on the size of the stump and how many you have:

  • Small stump (under 12"): 15–30 minutes
  • Medium stump (12"–24"): 30–60 minutes
  • Large stump (24"+): 1–2 hours or more
  • Multiple stumps: We often complete 3–5 average stumps in a half day

Setup, moving the machine into position, and cleanup add time on top of the actual grinding. A typical residential job with 1–2 stumps usually wraps up in well under 2 hours.

Standard practice is 6–12 inches below grade (ground level). For most homeowners who just want to mow over the area or plant grass, 6–8 inches is sufficient. If you plan to plant a new tree, install pavers, or pour concrete, we recommend grinding deeper — up to 12 inches — to remove as much wood as possible before it decomposes.

Stump grinding shreds the visible stump down below grade, leaving the root system underground to decompose naturally over 3–7 years. It's faster, less disruptive, and less expensive.

Stump removal extracts the entire root ball from the ground. It's more thorough but requires heavy equipment, leaves a large hole, and typically costs significantly more.

For most homeowners, grinding is the right choice. We recommend full removal only if you're installing a structure directly over the area or dealing with a species known for aggressive regrowth (like willows or poplars).

Lawn & Property

Minimal damage is expected — we use rubber-tired machines that distribute weight evenly across your turf. In most cases, any tire tracks or minor scuffing disappears after the first mowing. We can also place protective mats in front of the machine on vulnerable or wet ground.

If the ground is very soft or saturated, we may recommend rescheduling for a drier day to avoid ruts. As a general rule: if you can walk on your lawn without sinking in, our equipment won't cause lasting damage.

Our smaller grinders can fit through a standard 36-inch gate opening. For gates narrower than 36 inches, we may need to discuss options — including temporary fence panel removal or using hand tools for access.

After grinding, you'll have a pile of wood chips and sawdust where the stump was. You have a few options:

  • Fill the hole: We rake the chips back into the void — they compact over time and grass can grow over them.
  • Use as mulch: The chips make excellent garden mulch. We can spread them around nearby beds.
  • Haul away: If you want the area completely cleared, we can bag and remove the chips for an additional fee.
⚠️ Confirm chip hauling pricing and availability with the owner.

We grind the main stump and any major surface roots that are accessible. Roots that are far from the base and running just under the surface of the lawn (the kind that trip you while mowing) can also be addressed — just point them out when we arrive and we'll assess what we can grind.

Deep underground roots are not ground as part of a standard job — those are left to decompose naturally.

Roots & Regrowth

For most tree species, no. Once the stump is ground away, the tree has no way to photosynthesize and the root system gradually starves and dies.

However, some species — particularly poplars, willows, elms, and certain oaks — can send up new shoots from the surviving root system. If you notice sprouts appearing after grinding, they can be cut down repeatedly until the roots exhaust their stored energy (usually within 1–2 growing seasons) or treated with a stump-killer herbicide applied to fresh cuts.

💡 Tell us the tree species when you book — we can advise on regrowth risk upfront.

The underground root system is left in place and decomposes naturally over time. Small roots break down in a few years; large roots from mature trees can take 7–10 years to fully decompose. As they break down, the soil above them may settle slightly — this is normal and not a concern for most uses.

Decomposing roots temporarily pull nitrogen from the surrounding soil as the wood breaks down. If you're planting directly over the area soon after grinding, adding a nitrogen-rich fertilizer can help.

Once the tree is gone, the roots lose their energy source and stop growing. Any pressure the roots were exerting on nearby structures stops progressing from that point forward. Existing damage won't get worse, but the roots won't repair damage they've already caused either.

If roots are already pushing up a driveway section or cracking a sidewalk, those repairs are separate from the stump grinding job and would need to be addressed by a concrete contractor.

Preparation & Safety

A few things help the job go smoothly:

  • Clear any rocks, debris, or lawn decorations within about 5 feet of the stump — flying chips can travel
  • Make sure the gate is unlocked and the path to the stump is clear
  • Mark any sprinkler heads, low-voltage landscape lighting, or buried lines you're aware of near the stump
  • Move vehicles out of the driveway if access is needed
  • Let us know about any underground irrigation or invisible fence wires in the area

In most states, you are required by law to call 811 (the national "Call Before You Dig" line) at least 3 business days before any digging or grinding. 811 dispatches locators to mark the approximate location of underground utility lines — gas, electric, water, cable, and phone — with paint or flags.

We strongly recommend making this call before your appointment. It's free, it's quick, and it protects you, your yard, and our crew from accidentally striking a line.

💡 Call 811 or visit call811.com to request a locate. Allow 3 business days before your scheduled appointment.

After the Job

Yes, but wait a few weeks. Fresh wood chips are slow to decompose and can temporarily draw nitrogen out of the soil — which can stress new grass. For best results:

  • Remove as much of the chip pile as possible from the hole
  • Fill in with quality topsoil
  • Apply a nitrogen-rich starter fertilizer
  • Seed and water regularly

Grass planted after stump grinding usually establishes well within one growing season.

We recommend waiting at least 1–2 years before planting a new tree in the same location. The decomposing wood from the old stump and roots competes with a new tree's roots for nutrients and can harbor fungal diseases. If you want to replant sooner, excavate the old chip material, backfill with fresh topsoil, and choose a disease-resistant species.

Yes, this is completely normal. As the wood chips and remaining roots underground decompose, the ground above settles. It typically takes 1–3 years for the area to fully stabilize. You can speed up the process by:

  • Topping up the hole with extra topsoil as it settles
  • Watering regularly to help decomposition
  • Aerating the area in spring and fall

The settlement is rarely more than a few inches and has no impact on surrounding lawn health.

Not usually. Fungi are nature's decomposers — they're doing exactly what they're supposed to do, breaking down the remaining wood underground. Mushrooms appearing near a ground stump are almost always harmless wood-rotting species, not anything that will spread to your healthy lawn or trees.

They'll disappear on their own once the underground wood is fully decomposed. If you have pets or small children who might eat them, remove the mushroom caps promptly as a precaution.
